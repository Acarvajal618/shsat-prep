Unit 5_2

Factor Counting with Exclusion

Count multiples of A, subtract multiples of A×B.

Strategy

Core Approach
To find numbers divisible by 3 but NOT by 6, count all multiples of 3, then subtract multiples of 6 (since multiples of 6 are the ones divisible by BOTH 3 and 6). This is an inclusion-exclusion shortcut.

Step-by-Step Solution

  • 1
    Count multiples of 3 from 1–99: 99 ÷ 3 = 33 numbers.
  • 2
    Count multiples of 6 from 1–99: floor(99 ÷ 6) = 16 numbers.
  • 3
    Multiples of 3 but not 6: 33 − 16 = 17.
✅
Answer: D → 17
Check: odd multiples of 3 from 3,9,15,...99 — every other multiple of 3 is odd = 33/2 ≈ 17 odd ones ✓

Common Mistakes

  • ✗ Mistake Trying to list all numbers — too slow for the test.
  • ✗ Mistake Using 99÷6=16.5 and rounding up to 17 instead of down to 16.
  • 💡 Key Insight A number divisible by 6 must be divisible by both 2 and 3. So 'divisible by 3 but not 6' means 'divisible by 3 but not 2' — i.e., ODD multiples of 3.

When to use this

  • 📌 'Has factor X but not factor Y'
  • 📌 Large range of numbers — don't list them all
  • 📌 Use division to count: floor(n/k) = count of multiples of k up to n

Sample Problem

How many numbers from 1 to 99 have 3 as a factor but do NOT have 6 as a factor?

A 1
B 3
C 9
D 17 ✓
