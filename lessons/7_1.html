Area of a Quadrilateral via Diagonal

Split the quadrilateral into two right triangles using the diagonal, find each area, add.

The Problem

Quadrilateral ABCD has right angles at D and B. AD = 20 cm, DC = 15 cm, BC = 7 cm, AB = 24 cm. What is the area of quadrilateral ABCD?

A 66 sq cm
B 117 sq cm
C 234 sq cm ✓ answer
D 468 sq cm

The Strategy

Core idea

When a quadrilateral has right angles at two vertices, draw the diagonal connecting those vertices to create two right triangles. Find the area of each triangle (½ × base × height), then add them together.

When you'll see this

  • 🔍Right angles at two non-adjacent vertices → diagonal creates two right triangles.
  • 🔍Area of right triangle = ½ × leg₁ × leg₂.
  • 🔍If the answer choices include double your answer, check you used ½ for triangle area.

Step-by-Step Solution

  1. 1
    Draw diagonal AC to split ABCD into two right triangles.
    Triangle ADC has legs AD = 20 and DC = 15 (right angle at D).
    Triangle ABC has legs BC = 7 and AB = 24 (right angle at B).
  2. 2
    Find area of triangle ADC.
    Area = ½ × 20 × 15 = ½ × 300 = 150 sq cm
  3. 3
    Find area of triangle ABC.
    Area = ½ × 7 × 24 = ½ × 168 = 84 sq cm
  4. 4
    Add the two areas.
    150 + 84 = 234 sq cm
✅
234 sq cm → Answer: C
Check: ½(20)(15) = 150, ½(7)(24) = 84. Total = 234 ✓

Common Mistakes

  • ❌
    Forgetting the ½ in triangle area Computing 20×15 + 7×24 = 300 + 168 = 468 — that's answer D (double the correct answer). Always use ½ for triangle area.
  • ❌
    Only calculating one triangle Getting 150 or 84 alone. The quadrilateral = BOTH triangles combined.
  • ❌
    Using wrong pairs of legs Pairing AD with BC (different triangles) instead of AD with DC, and BC with AB.
