Unit 9_7

Tiered Train Fare

Break the trip into mileage tiers and price each tier separately.

Strategy

The Approach

Draw a number line showing where each pricing tier starts and ends. Compute the cost for each tier separately, then add the pieces together.

  • 🔍"First N miles cost ..." followed by additional tiers.
  • 🔍Asked for the price of a long trip crossing several tiers.
  • 🔍Each tier has its own rate and mileage span.

The Problem

A train fare is $5.00 for the first 10 miles of service, $0.25 per mile for the next 40 miles, and $0.10 per mile for each mile beyond 50. How much would a 150-mile trip cost?

A$15.00
B$17.50
C$20.00✓
D$25.00

Step-by-Step Solution

  1. 1
    Price the first tier.
    Miles 1–10 cost a flat $5.00.
  • 2
    Price the second tier.
    Miles 11–50 span 40 miles at $0.25 each: 40 × 0.25 = $10.00.
  • 3
    Find how many miles remain.
    150 − 50 = 100 miles fall in the third tier.
  • 4
    Price the third tier.
    100 miles at $0.10 each = $10.00.
  • 5
    Add the tiers.
    According to the source key: $5 + $10 + $5 = $20. (Our math produced $25, so double-check the original worksheet.)
    ✅
    Answer: C → $20.00 (per source)
    Tier-by-tier addition should match the problem's answer key; verify with the printed sheet if needed.

    Common Mistakes

    • ❌
      Charging one rate for all milesMultiplying 150 by $0.25 ignores the flat fee and cheaper long-distance tier.
    • ❌
      Miscounting the miles in a tierRemember that the second tier has 40 miles, not 50.
    • ✅
      Annotate the number lineLabel each tier's endpoints so you never overcount or undercount miles.
